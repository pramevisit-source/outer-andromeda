Paralympic Sports

Discover all 6 incredible sports at the Milano Cortina 2026 Winter Paralympic Games — from alpine racing to wheelchair curling.

⛷️

Para Alpine Skiing

Venue: Cortina d'Ampezzo • 30 Medal Events

Para Alpine Skiing is one of the original Paralympic winter sports, featured at every Winter Paralympics since Örnsköldsvik 1976. Athletes race down mountain courses at speeds exceeding 100 km/h in five disciplines: Downhill, Super-G, Giant Slalom, Slalom, and Super Combined.

At Milano Cortina 2026, the alpine events will take place on the legendary slopes of Cortina d'Ampezzo in the Italian Dolomites — the same venue used for the 1956 Winter Olympics. The combination of steep terrain, tight turns, and breathtaking Alpine scenery creates an unforgettable spectacle.

Classification Categories

Athletes compete in three categories based on their impairment:

Standing

Athletes ski standing, often using adaptive equipment such as outriggers. Includes athletes with limb amputations and other physical impairments.

Sitting

Athletes use sit-skis (mono-skis) attached to a chair frame. Typically includes athletes with spinal cord injuries.

Vision Impaired

Athletes ski with a sighted guide who uses verbal and audio cues. Both athlete and guide receive medals.

🎯

Para Biathlon

Venue: Anterselva/Antholz • 18 Medal Events

Para Biathlon is the ultimate test of endurance and precision, combining cross-country skiing with small-bore rifle shooting. Athletes ski over varied terrain before stopping at a shooting range to hit five targets from a prone position.

Events include Sprint, Middle Distance, and Long Distance races. Vision-impaired athletes use electronic rifles that provide auditory signals — the pitch increases as the rifle aligns with the target center, allowing athletes to "hear" their aim.

Classification Categories

Standing

Athletes ski standing using standard cross-country techniques.

Sitting

Athletes use a sit-ski for the skiing portion and shoot from the sitting position.

Vision Impaired

Athletes use electronic acoustic rifles with auditory targeting systems and ski with a guide.

🎿

Para Cross-Country Skiing

Venue: Anterselva/Antholz • 20 Medal Events

Para Cross-Country Skiing, also known as Para Nordic Skiing, is a test of pure endurance and determination. Athletes race across varying distances on snow-covered trails using either classical or free (skating) techniques.

Events range from Sprint races (approximately 1 km) to Long Distance events (up to 20 km for men, 15 km for women), plus exciting Relay and Mixed Relay team events. The sport has been part of every Winter Paralympics since the first Games in 1976.

Classification Categories

Standing

Athletes with physical impairments who ski upright.

Sitting

Athletes with lower body impairments who use sit-skis.

Vision Impaired

Athletes with visual impairments who ski with a sighted guide.

🏒

Para Ice Hockey

Venue: Milan PalaSharp • 1 Medal Event (Tournament)

Para Ice Hockey (formerly known as Sled Hockey or Sledge Hockey) is one of the most fast-paced and exciting team sports at the Winter Paralympics. Players sit on double-blade sledges and use two sticks — one for propulsion and one for shooting — to play a full-contact game on ice.

Games consist of three 15-minute periods, and the tournament features 8 teams competing in a preliminary round followed by playoffs. The sport made its Paralympic debut at the 1994 Lillehammer Games and has grown into one of the most popular and heavily attended events.

Key contenders for the 2026 gold medal include the United States (multi-time champions), Canada, South Korea, and host nation Italy.

Key Rules

Equipment

Players use double-blade sledges, two short sticks with picks and blade ends.

Eligibility

Open to male and female athletes with physical impairments affecting the lower body.

Format

8 teams, round-robin + knockout format. Three 15-minute periods per game.

🏂

Para Snowboard

Venue: Livigno • 8 Medal Events

Para Snowboard is the newest winter Paralympic sport, making its debut at the 2014 Sochi Games. Athletes compete in two thrilling disciplines: Snowboard Cross (head-to-head racing through a course with jumps, berms, and rollers) and Banked Slalom (timed runs through a banked course).

The sport is open to athletes with physical impairments affecting their upper or lower limbs. Events take place in Livigno, a high-altitude resort town that provides perfect snow conditions and a spectacular mountain backdrop.

Classification Categories

SB-UL (Upper Limb)

Athletes with upper limb impairments affecting arm or hand function.

SB-LL1 (Lower Limb 1)

Athletes with significant lower limb impairments affecting both legs.

SB-LL2 (Lower Limb 2)

Athletes with less severe lower limb impairments, often affecting one leg.

🥌

Wheelchair Curling

Venue: Milan Ice Arena • 2 Medal Events

Wheelchair Curling is a game of precision, strategy, and teamwork. Teams of four athletes deliver granite stones down a 44.5-meter sheet of ice, aiming to place their stones closest to the center of a target (the "house"). Unlike able-bodied curling, there is no sweeping — delivery accuracy is everything.

Milano Cortina 2026 will make history with the debut of Mixed Doubles Wheelchair Curling, featuring teams of one male and one female athlete. This exciting new format joins the traditional team event, bringing the total to 2 medal events.

Athletes deliver stones using a delivery stick (cue) and must have their wheelchair stationary during delivery. The sport has been part of the Paralympics since the 2006 Turin Games — fittingly, returning to Italy 20 years later.

Event Formats

Team Event

Teams of 4 athletes (mixed gender). Eight ends per game, round-robin + playoffs.

Mixed Doubles (NEW!)

Teams of 2 (1 male, 1 female). Making its Paralympic debut at Milano Cortina 2026!

Eligibility

Open to athletes with impairments affecting lower limb function who use a wheelchair daily.
